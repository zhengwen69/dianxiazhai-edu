# Technology-Assisted Self-Directed Professional Development for Cross-Disciplinary Vocational Teachers: A Mixed-Methods Study from Chinese UAV Education

---

## Abstract

**Background**: The rapid expansion of vocational education and training (VET) worldwide has created a structural shortage of discipline-matched teachers, forcing large numbers of vocational educators into cross-disciplinary teaching assignments for which they lack formal preparation. In China's unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) vocational education sector, which has grown to over 200 programmes since the 2019 national VET reform, most teachers have been reassigned from adjacent fields such as surveying, mechanical engineering, and electronics. Traditional professional development models—off-site training, degree study, and school-based mentoring—prove insufficient for this population due to cost, time, and the absence of UAV-specific expertise within institutions. This study empirically examines a zero-cost, self-directed, technology-assisted professional development model (NFSC+Agent) designed to address this gap.

**Methods**: A convergent parallel mixed-methods design was employed, integrating three data modules: (A) administrative background surveys (target N ≥ 20) profiling Sichuan province UAV colleges; (B) peer teacher evaluations (target N ≥ 40) from 30+ institutions, using a 15-item Likert instrument across seven dimensions plus two open-ended questions; and (C) student usability questionnaires (target N ≥ 60) comprising the System Usability Scale (SUS), Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) subscales, and agent satisfaction items. Longitudinal teaching archives spanning 10 semesters (2021–2025) provided process evidence. Quantitative data were analysed using descriptive statistics, independent-sample t-tests, and Cronbach's alpha for internal consistency; qualitative open-ended responses were analysed using thematic analysis.

## §1 Introduction

The global expansion of vocational education and training (VET) has outpaced the supply of qualified teachers, producing a structural phenomenon in which substantial numbers of VET educators teach outside their formal disciplinary training (Ingersoll, 2003; Misra, 2011). The OECD has identified teacher shortages as one of the most persistent challenges facing VET systems internationally, particularly in rapidly industrialising economies where new technical programmes are launched before a corresponding teacher workforce can be cultivated (OECD, 2021). In China, this challenge is acute: the 2019 National Vocational Education Reform Implementation Plan (State Council, 2019) catalysed a wave of programme expansion across higher vocational colleges, with emerging technical disciplines such as unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) application technology proliferating from fewer than 50 programmes in 2017 to over 200 by 2023. The speed of this expansion has created an unprecedented demand for UAV-specialist teachers that the existing teacher education pipeline cannot meet.

Sichuan province exemplifies the structural dynamics at play. As a major UAV industrial base—hosting manufacturers, testing ranges, and agricultural UAV application zones—the province has seen aggressive UAV programme launches across its higher vocational colleges. Yet a recent capacity-mapping exercise conducted by the research group revealed that fewer than one in ten UAV teachers across Sichuan VET institutions possessed formal UAV academic training. The overwhelming majority had been reassigned from surveying and mapping, mechanical engineering, electronics, or agronomy backgrounds. These cross-disciplinary teachers enter UAV classrooms with domain knowledge from their original disciplines but without systematic preparation in UAV-specific content, industry standards, equipment operation, or safety protocols. They are, by institutional mandate, required to function as UAV subject-matter experts while simultaneously constructing that expertise through self-directed means.

The professional development infrastructure available to these teachers is constrained. Off-site training programmes offered by UAV manufacturers or industry associations are intermittent, costly, and typically accommodate only one or two teachers per institution per year. Graduate degree study in UAV-related fields, while theoretically available, imposes prohibitive time costs on in-service teachers who carry full teaching loads of 16–20 contact hours per week. School-based professional development, the default mechanism in most vocational colleges, is designed around mentoring by experienced colleagues within the same discipline—a resource that, by definition, does not exist for newly launched UAV programmes where no senior UAV teacher is present. The resulting situation is one of institutionalised improvisation: teachers must become UAV-competent largely through their own efforts, with minimal institutional support.

Technology-assisted self-directed learning has been proposed as a potentially transformative approach for teacher professional development in resource-constrained contexts (Fishman et al., 2013; Trust & Horrocks, 2019), yet systematic empirical evidence of its application to cross-disciplinary VET teachers remains absent from the literature. This paper addresses that gap by reporting a mixed-methods empirical investigation of the NFSC+Agent self-directed development model—a zero-cost, technology-assisted approach in which a structured four-phase self-study methodology (Narrative–Framework–Scene–Creation, NFSC) is combined with a suite of nine role-specialised artificial intelligence agents to scaffold cross-disciplinary learning. The model was implemented over five years (2021–2025) in the context of Chinese UAV vocational education, resulting in eight fully developed UAV courses across four categories (control, theory, application, data) and a web-based learning portal (CD-UAV) that serves both teacher self-study and student companion functions.

This study was guided by two research questions:

- **RQ1**: How does the NFSC+Agent self-directed model support cross-disciplinary VET teacher development, as evidenced by longitudinal teaching practice and learner-perceived usability?
- **RQ2**: How do external peer teachers perceive the model's pedagogical effectiveness and transferability to other VET institutions?

The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 reviews the intersecting literatures on cross-disciplinary teaching in VET, self-directed teacher professional development, technology-supported teacher learning, and competence measurement in VET research. Section 3 details the mixed-methods design, participants, instruments, and analytical procedures. Section 4 presents results from the three data modules. Section 5 discusses findings in relation to the research questions and existing literature, and Section 6 concludes with contributions, limitations, and future directions.

## §2 Literature Review

### 2.1 Cross-Disciplinary Teaching in Vocational Education and Training

Cross-disciplinary teaching—also described as out-of-field teaching—refers to the assignment of teachers to subjects for which they lack formal qualification. Ingersoll's (2003) landmark analysis established that out-of-field teaching is not a temporary remedial measure but a structural feature of education systems worldwide, driven by mismatches between teacher supply and curriculum demand. In VET, this phenomenon takes on additional dimensions of complexity. Unlike general education, where out-of-field teaching primarily involves content knowledge gaps, VET cross-disciplinary teaching also requires mastery of industry-specific procedural knowledge, equipment operation protocols, safety standards, and certification frameworks (Grollmann, 2008; Billett, 2014). A mechanical engineer reassigned to teach UAV maintenance, for example, must acquire not only aeronautical knowledge but also the regulatory framework of the Civil Aviation Administration of China (CAAC), practical flight operation skills, and familiarity with rapid technological iteration in the UAV industry.

Akkerman and Bakker's (2011) theory of boundary crossing provides a productive lens for understanding this transition. Boundary crossing describes the process by which individuals navigate between different sociocultural practices, negotiating discontinuities in norms, tools, and knowledge structures. For cross-disciplinary VET teachers, the boundary between their original discipline and UAV technology constitutes a learning challenge that neither traditional formal education nor conventional short-term training fully addresses. The boundary crossing framework suggests that effective transitions require "boundary objects"—artefacts that inhabit multiple intersecting social worlds and facilitate communication and learning across them. In the context of this study, the CD-UAV portal and its integrated NFSC+Agent system function as such a boundary object, translating between the teacher's prior knowledge domain and the target UAV teaching domain.

Recent empirical work in ER-VET has advanced the measurement and conceptualisation of VET teacher competence. Bottling et al. (2024), in an 18-month longitudinal study of VET teacher training in Germany, demonstrated that motivational and emotional states significantly influence participation trajectories, suggesting that self-directed models must account for affective dimensions of learning, not merely cognitive ones. Ma et al. (2024) explored the interplay between vocational competence and dropout intention among apprentices, underscoring the importance of competence development pathways that maintain learner engagement—a finding with direct implications for teacher self-study design.

### 2.2 Self-Directed Teacher Professional Development

The concept of self-directed learning, articulated by Knowles (1975) as a process "in which individuals take the initiative, with or without the help of others, in diagnosing their learning needs, formulating learning goals, identifying human and material resources for learning, choosing and implementing appropriate learning strategies, and evaluating learning outcomes," has been central to adult education theory for five decades. Brookfield (1986) extended this framework by emphasising the critical reflective dimension: self-directed learners not only manage the logistics of their learning but also critically examine the assumptions underlying their practice—a capacity particularly important for teachers building competence in unfamiliar disciplines where established pedagogical scripts do not exist.

Eraut's (2004) research on informal workplace learning is directly relevant to the cross-disciplinary VET teacher scenario. Eraut distinguished between deliberative learning (planned, goal-directed), reactive learning (spontaneous reflection on experience), and implicit learning (unconscious absorption of workplace practices). The NFSC+Agent model aims to convert what would otherwise remain implicit or reactive learning into deliberative, structured self-study—transforming the workplace from a site of ad hoc adaptation into a designed learning environment. This is consistent with Billett's (2014) concept of "learning in the circumstances of practice," which holds that workplace learning can be optimised through intentional structuring of practice experiences and guided access to expert knowledge.

Within the VET-specific literature, Dymock and Tyler (2018) argued for a more systematic approach to continuing professional development (CPD) in VET, noting that the sector has historically relied on episodic, supply-driven training that poorly serves teachers in specialised or emerging technical fields. Their call for "demand-driven," workplace-embedded CPD models aligns with the self-directed logic of the NFSC+Agent approach. Deissinger and Gonon (2021) further observed that the cultural foundations of VET systems shape teacher identity and professional development expectations: in apprenticeship-strong systems such as Germany and Switzerland, the master–apprentice model influences teacher development expectations, whereas in school-based VET systems such as China's, institutional mechanisms for teacher learning must be designed differently.

### 2.3 Technology-Supported Teacher Learning

The integration of technology into teacher professional development has evolved from early uses of learning management systems (LMS) for content delivery to more sophisticated applications of artificial intelligence and adaptive learning (Fishman et al., 2013; Voogt & McKenney, 2017). Kali et al. (2015) proposed that teachers function as "designers of technology-enhanced learning," actively shaping digital tools to their pedagogical contexts rather than passively receiving pre-packaged solutions. This designer stance is reflected in the NFSC+Agent model, where teachers populate agent templates with discipline-specific content and adapt the platform architecture to their own teaching needs.

In the VET sector specifically, technology adoption research has identified distinctive barriers. Zubizarreta Pagaldai et al. (2025) found in their study of basic VET students in Spain that digital competence is shaped by an interplay of institutional infrastructure, teacher modelling, and curriculum integration—factors that apply equally to VET teachers' own learning with technology. Gentner et al. (2026) compared trainee and in-service teachers' implementation of simulation-based learning environments, finding that in-service teachers may be more receptive to technology adoption when it directly addresses practical teaching challenges—a finding that supports the positioning of NFSC+Agent as a response to the concrete problem of cross-disciplinary teaching competence, not a technology adoption initiative per se.

The role of artificial intelligence agents in teacher learning is an emerging research frontier. Pargmann et al. (2025), in a study published in ER-VET, demonstrated that AI-generated feedback could support vocational teacher trainees' lesson planning in a longitudinal intervention design. Their finding that "analytical AI platforms" can provide structure and feedback in the absence of human mentors has direct relevance to the present study, where the nine-specialised-agent system functions as a distributed cognitive scaffold for the cross-disciplinary teacher. Methodologically, Pargmann et al. employed a longitudinal intervention design with repeated measures, which this study extends by adding the multi-stakeholder evaluation dimension (peer teachers and students) that the authors identified as a limitation of their single-stakeholder design.

Similarly, Bottling et al. (2024) traced VET teacher trainees' participation trajectories over 18 months in an ER-VET study, finding that motivational and emotional states significantly influenced sustained engagement—a finding with implications for the self-directed model under investigation, which relies heavily on sustained teacher motivation over years rather than months. Gentner et al. (2026) compared trainee and in-service teachers' implementation of simulation-based learning, demonstrating that in-service teachers' greater practical teaching experience influenced their technology adoption patterns differently from trainees—a distinction relevant to the present study's focus on practising (rather than pre-service) cross-disciplinary teachers. Collectively, these ER-VET studies establish a methodological and substantive landscape within which the present investigation is positioned: longitudinal designs with multiple measurement points, attention to motivational/affective dimensions of technology-supported learning, and sensitivity to the practising-versus-trainee distinction in VET teacher populations.

### 2.4 Competence Measurement and Test Instrument Validation in VET

The validation of assessment instruments is an explicitly articulated scope area for ER-VET and a methodological priority for this study. Shulman's (1987) pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) framework—which posits that effective teaching requires the integration of content knowledge with pedagogical knowledge specific to the teaching of that content—has been extended to VET contexts where "content knowledge" encompasses both theoretical understanding and procedural industry competence (Rauner & Maclean, 2008). Validating instruments that measure both the teacher's self-study process and its learner-perceived outcomes therefore requires triangulation across multiple stakeholder perspectives.

The System Usability Scale (SUS), developed by Brooke (1996), has become one of the most widely used standardised instruments for evaluating the usability of technology systems. Its ten-item, five-point Likert format yields a composite score on a 0–100 scale, with established benchmarks: scores above 68 are considered above average, and scores above 80.3 are in the "excellent" range (Sauro & Lewis, 2016). The SUS has been applied in educational technology contexts globally, though its use in VET-specific technology evaluation remains relatively uncommon, representing a methodological contribution of this study.

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), originating from Davis (1989), measures two core constructs—perceived usefulness (PU) and perceived ease of use (PEOU)—that predict user adoption of information technology. Venkatesh and Bala (2008) extended the TAM framework (TAM3) to incorporate additional determinants, but the PU–PEOU core remains the most parsimonious and widely validated model for technology acceptance research. In this study, TAM items were adapted to the CD-UAV portal context to capture students' perceptions of the platform's usefulness for UAV learning and its ease of use.

The peer evaluation instrument developed for this study—a 15-item Likert scale covering seven dimensions of the NFSC+Agent model—was designed following established principles for educational instrument development (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2017). Recent ER-VET publications have modelled rigorous instrument validation approaches: Meijer et al. (2025) mapped teachers' perceptions of assessment competence in Dutch higher VET using factor-analytic methods, and Welsandt et al. (2024) developed and validated a technology-based assessment of economic literacy with multi-stage expert review and pilot testing. This study adopts analogous validation procedures, reporting Cronbach's alpha for all instruments and subjecting the peer evaluation instrument to expert review prior to field deployment.

### 2.5 Research Gap

The preceding review identifies a convergence point that remains unexplored in the empirical literature: the intersection of cross-disciplinary VET teacher development, self-directed learning supported by zero-cost technology (including AI agents), and multi-stakeholder empirical validation combining peer teacher evaluation and standardised usability instruments. While each of these domains has received individual scholarly attention, no study has examined how a self-directed, technology-assisted model functions when deployed for a single cross-disciplinary VET teacher and then evaluated by external peer teachers from multiple institutions. This gap is both theoretical—understanding the mechanisms by which boundary crossing is supported by structured self-study protocols—and practical—determining whether such a model can be transferred to other VET institutions facing similar cross-disciplinary staffing challenges.

## §3 Methodology

### 3.1 Research Design

This study employed a convergent parallel mixed-methods design (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2017), integrating quantitative and qualitative data collected simultaneously from three distinct respondent groups. The quantitative strand was prioritised, with qualitative data serving a complementary function to contextualise and enrich numerical findings. This design was selected for its alignment with ER-VET's emphasis on empirical investigation and instrument validation, and for its capacity to address both the effectiveness of the intervention (RQ1, primarily through Module C student data and Module A institutional context) and its perceived transferability (RQ2, primarily through Module B peer teacher data).

The intervention under evaluation—the NFSC+Agent self-directed development model—served as the independent condition. The dependent measures comprised: (a) peer teachers' evaluations of the model's pedagogical quality and potential for adoption (15-item Likert scale, seven dimensions); (b) students' perceived usability and technology acceptance of the CD-UAV learning portal (SUS, TAM, and agent satisfaction items); and (c) institutional administrators' characterisation of the cross-disciplinary teaching context (Module A background survey). Longitudinal teaching archives (10 semesters, 2021–2025) provided process evidence to contextualise the intervention's developmental trajectory.

### 3.2 The Intervention: NFSC+Agent Self-Directed Development Model

The NFSC+Agent model—previously described in the research programme literature (Author, 2025)—comprises four integrated components. First, the NFSC methodology provides a structured four-phase self-study protocol: Narrative (motivation triggering through authentic problem scenarios), Framework (systematic knowledge mapping across the target discipline), Scene (competence cultivation through situated practice), and Creation (production of usable teaching outputs). Second, a nine-role agent system, originally developed for a solid waste engineering course and subsequently migrated to the UAV domain, supplies role-specialised scaffolding: agents function variously as knowledge architects, practice-simulators, regulation-checkers, progress-trackers, and career-advisors. Third, the CD-UAV web portal—a zero-cost, dual-card homepage serving both teacher self-study and student companion functions—organises eight courses across four categories (control, theory, application, and data) within a six-module student interface (course navigation, agent companion, methodology comparison, growth data, licence examination, and career pathways). Fourth, a longitudinal development arc spanning 10 semesters (2021–2025) documents the evolution from a single pilot course to a complete eight-course UAV curriculum, during which the teacher—Teacher Z—transitioned from a surveying and environmental science background to full UAV teaching competence. Three teaching reform research projects and a copyright registration were completed during this period.

#### 3.2.1 Longitudinal Teaching Archives

The teaching archives comprise: (a) course syllabi and iterative revision records for all eight UAV courses; (b) teaching logs documenting weekly pedagogical decisions, challenges encountered, and adaptations made; (c) student assessment records including examination scores, licence examination pass rates, and competition results; and (d) records of the three teaching reform research projects and copyright registration. These archives serve as process evidence that contextualises the survey findings but are not presented as an independent empirical pillar in the Results section. They are referenced in the Discussion to illustrate the developmental trajectory that the quantitative data partially capture.

### 3.3 Participants and Sampling

Three respondent groups were recruited using purposive and convenience sampling strategies, reflecting the practical constraints of accessing a geographically dispersed and institutionally diverse population of UAV vocational educators in Sichuan province. Table 1 summarises the sampling frame.

All participation was voluntary, and informed consent was obtained prior to data collection.

### 3.4 Instruments

**Module A — Institutional Background Survey.** A 10-item structured questionnaire collected data on: institutional type and size; year of UAV programme launch; teacher headcount and proportion with UAV-specific academic backgrounds; cross-disciplinary teaching assignment rates; existing professional development mechanisms; and current AI tool usage among UAV teaching staff. Two open-ended items invited respondents to describe perceived challenges in cross-disciplinary teacher development at their institution.

**Module B — Peer Teacher Evaluation Instrument.** A 15-item Likert-scale questionnaire (1 = Strongly Disagree to 5 = Strongly Agree) was developed specifically for this study, organised into seven evaluative dimensions mapped to the CD-UAV portal's six student modules plus an overall transferability assessment:

- *Course Navigation* (B1–B2): Perceived clarity and comprehensiveness of course categorisation and sequencing.
- *Agent Companion* (B3–B5): Perceived pedagogical utility of the nine-role agent system for self-study scaffolding.
- *Methodology Comparison* (B6–B7): Perceived alignment of NFSC methodology with VET teaching requirements.
- *Licence System* (B8–B9): Perceived adequacy of licence examination support.
- *Career Pathways* (B10–B11): Perceived relevance of employment direction mapping.
- *Growth Data* (B12): Perceived utility of student progress tracking features.
- *Transferability* (B13): Overall judgement on whether the model could be adapted to the respondent's own institution and discipline.

Two open-ended questions (B14–B15) invited respondents to identify the model's main strengths and suggest improvements or necessary adaptations for transfer.

The peer evaluation instrument was developed through a three-stage process aligned with ER-VET's emphasis on test validation. In Stage 1, a pool of 20 candidate items was generated through deductive reasoning from the CD-UAV portal's six-module architecture and the NFSC theoretical framework. In Stage 2, three VET researchers and two practising UAV vocational teachers reviewed the item pool for content validity, clarity, and domain coverage; items with content validity indices (CVI) below 0.80 were revised or eliminated, yielding the final 15-item instrument. In Stage 3 (pilot administration), the instrument was administered to a convenience sample of UAV teachers (N = [DATA PENDING]) to assess internal consistency (Cronbach's α) and item-total correlations; full psychometric properties will be reported upon completion of the main data collection. This instrument is therefore positioned as a pilot-validated tool whose psychometric refinement will continue as the sample accumulates.

**Module C — Student Usability Instruments.** Three standardised instruments were combined in a 21-item questionnaire. The System Usability Scale (Brooke, 1996), comprising 10 items on a 5-point Likert scale, was administered in a validated Chinese translation. TAM subscales (Davis, 1989) included four items for Perceived Usefulness and four items for Perceived Ease of Use, each on a 5-point Likert scale. A three-item Agent Satisfaction scale (custom-developed) assessed students' perceptions of the AI agent's helpfulness, supportiveness during difficulties, and overall contribution to the learning experience. Two open-ended items invited students to describe the most and least useful aspects of the CD-UAV portal.

### 3.5 Data Collection Procedure

Data collection was scheduled for June–July 2026, coordinated by the research group through field visits to Sichuan UAV vocational colleges and parallel online distribution.

1. **Field visits (Modules A and B)**: A member of the research group visited UAV vocational colleges across Sichuan province. During each visit, institutional administrators completed the Module A background survey, and peer teachers were invited to explore the CD-UAV portal on-site before completing the Module B evaluation questionnaire. Teachers who could not complete the questionnaire during the visit received a WeChat link for follow-up completion.
2. **Online survey (Module C)**: The student questionnaire was distributed via Wenjuanxing (问卷星), a widely used Chinese online survey platform. Survey settings enforced one response per device and required all Likert items before submission.
3. **Archival compilation**: Teaching archives from 2021–2025 were collated, chronologically organised, and reviewed for completeness prior to qualitative analysis.

### 3.6 Data Analysis

Quantitative data were analysed using SPSS. Descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation, frequency distributions) were computed for all Likert-scale items. The SUS composite score was calculated using Brooke's (1996) formula: each item's score contribution = (raw score − 1) for odd-numbered items and (5 − raw score) for even-numbered items; the sum of contributions was multiplied by 2.5 to yield a 0–100 scale score. TAM subscale means were computed for perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use.

Subgroup comparisons were conducted using independent-sample t-tests: peer teachers with versus without UAV-specific academic backgrounds (Module B), and students with versus without UAV pilot licences and across grade levels (Module C). Internal consistency was assessed using Cronbach's alpha, with α ≥ 0.70 considered acceptable.

Qualitative data from open-ended questionnaire items were analysed using thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase procedure: familiarisation, initial coding, theme generation, theme review, theme definition, and report writing. Two coders independently coded responses, with disagreements resolved through discussion.

### 3.7 Ethical Considerations

Ethical approval was obtained from the institutional review board of [University A]. All participants provided informed consent, with questionnaire completion constituting implicit consent after reading a participant information sheet. All responses were anonymised: peer teachers and administrators were assigned institutional codes, and student responses were collected without personal identifiers. Data are stored on password-protected institutional servers, accessible only to the research team. The study posed minimal risk to participants and did not involve vulnerable populations.

## §5 Discussion

### 5.1 RQ1: Effectiveness of the NFSC+Agent Self-Directed Model

The first research question asked how the NFSC+Agent self-directed model supports cross-disciplinary VET teacher development, as evidenced by longitudinal teaching practice and learner-perceived usability. The longitudinal teaching archives (2021–2025) provide the most substantial evidence currently available. Over ten semesters, Teacher Z—whose original disciplinary grounding was in surveying and environmental science—developed a complete eight-course UAV curriculum spanning four categories (control, theory, application, and data), established a zero-cost web portal serving dual teacher self-study and student companion functions, completed three teaching reform research projects, and obtained a copyright registration. This trajectory, from a single pilot course in the first semester to a functionally complete curriculum by the tenth, constitutes prima facie evidence that the NFSC+Agent model can support the knowledge acquisition, pedagogical adaptation, and sustained motivation necessary for cross-disciplinary teaching competence.

The CD-UAV portal's six-module structure reflects an intentional translation of the NFSC methodology into a student-facing interface: course navigation (Framework phase), agent companion (Scene phase through simulated practice and feedback), methodology comparison (meta-cognitive reflection across educational levels), growth data (Creation phase through tangible learning evidence), and licence and career modules (Narrative phase through future-oriented motivation). This architectural mapping between the self-study methodology and the student platform is not incidental; it represents the dual-use logic of the model—the same cognitive structure that scaffolds the teacher's own learning is externalised into a platform that scaffolds student learning. External evaluators reviewing the platform can therefore infer the quality of the teacher's internal knowledge organisation from the platform's pedagogical coherence, a logic that underlies the Module B peer evaluation design.

Student usability data will, upon collection, provide the learner-side validation of this architecture. The SUS instrument is particularly informative: a score above the 68-point "above average" benchmark would indicate that students find the platform usable and would be likely to continue using it voluntarily. The anticipated SUS mean of approximately 74, while modest by commercial software standards, would represent a reasonable outcome for a zero-cost, single-developer educational platform and would, more importantly, signal that the cross-disciplinary teacher's self-constructed learning materials meet a basic threshold of learner-perceived quality. The TAM subscales will disaggregate this assessment into usefulness and ease-of-use components, potentially revealing specific areas for platform refinement.

### 5.2 RQ2: Peer-Perceived Transferability

The second research question asked how external peer teachers perceive the model's pedagogical effectiveness and transferability to other VET institutions. The seven-dimension peer evaluation instrument was designed to decompose the global "this works" judgement into functionally distinct assessments corresponding to the CD-UAV portal's modules. The dimension most directly addressing RQ2—Transferability (B13)—asks respondents to judge whether the model could be adapted to their own institution and discipline, capturing the practical applicability that ER-VET readers (policymakers, institutional leaders, and teacher educators) most need to assess.

A notable design feature of the subgroup comparison is its alignment with boundary crossing theory. Teachers who lack UAV-specific academic backgrounds—the model's primary intended users—are expected to evaluate the model differently from those with formal UAV training. If non-UAV-background teachers rate the Agent Companion and Methodology Comparison dimensions significantly higher than their UAV-background peers, this would suggest that the model's structured self-study protocol and distributed agent scaffolding are perceived as particularly valuable by those who, like Teacher Z, must cross disciplinary boundaries to achieve teaching competence. Such a finding would be consistent with Akkerman and Bakker's (2011) proposition that boundary objects—here, the CD-UAV portal and its embedded agents—facilitate learning across sociocultural discontinuities precisely because they render unfamiliar knowledge structures accessible through familiar cognitive frames.

Conversely, if UAV-background teachers rate Course Navigation or Licence System dimensions higher (perhaps due to their ability to benchmark these against industry standards), the pattern would suggest a different mechanism: domain experts validate the model's content accuracy while boundary-crossers validate its pedagogical scaffolding. Both patterns carry implications for transfer: the model may need to be robust on content (validated by disciplinary insiders) and accessible in process (validated by boundary-crossers) to succeed in new institutional contexts.

### 5.3 Cross-Disciplinary Transfer Conditions

Synthesising the teaching archive evidence with the peer evaluation framework suggests three conditions for successful transfer of a self-directed, technology-assisted VET teacher development model. First, an **anchoring knowledge domain** is necessary: the teacher's existing disciplinary expertise must provide a cognitive bridge to the target domain. Teacher Z's background in surveying—with its spatial reasoning, coordinate systems, and field data collection methods—offered conceptual anchors for UAV flight planning, aerial photogrammetry, and remote sensing. A teacher whose original discipline lacks such structural commonalities with UAV technology (e.g., language education) would likely face a steeper learning curve. This anchoring condition implies that cross-disciplinary transfer is not universal but depends on the proximity between the source and target knowledge domains.

Second, a **structured self-study protocol** is essential. The NFSC four-phase sequence—Narrative, Framework, Scene, Creation—converts the amorphous challenge of "learning UAV technology" into a manageable sequence of cognitive operations: identifying authentic problems, mapping the knowledge terrain, practising in situated contexts, and producing tangible outputs. Without such structure, cross-disciplinary self-study risks degenerating into unsystematic information consumption, producing the "fragmented knowledge" that Eraut (2004) identified as a common pitfall of informal workplace learning. The peer evaluation dimension of Methodology Comparison (B6–B7) is designed to assess whether external teachers recognise this structured protocol as pedagogically coherent and replicable.

Third, **institutional tolerance** for self-directed experimentation is a precondition. The five-year developmental arc documented in the teaching archives occurred within an institutional environment that permitted Teacher Z to iteratively develop courses, experiment with agent configurations, and build the CD-UAV portal without immediate accountability to standardised teaching quality metrics. Whether other institutions—particularly those with more rigid curriculum approval processes or stronger top-down professional development cultures—can replicate this tolerance is an empirical question that the Transferability dimension (B13) and the administrative background survey (Module A) are designed to partially address.

### 5.4 Practical Implications

The practical significance of this study centres on the model's zero-cost architecture. In a global VET landscape where resource constraints are the norm rather than the exception—particularly in developing economies and in newly launched technical programmes—professional development models that require significant financial investment in external training, commercial platforms, or dedicated specialist positions are structurally inaccessible to the institutions that need them most (OECD, 2021). The NFSC+Agent model was developed and deployed using freely available web hosting, open-access knowledge resources, and a teacher's existing computing infrastructure. Its replication requires no licensing fees, no institutional procurement processes, and no dedicated IT support beyond basic web access. This zero-cost characteristic positions the model as a pragmatic bridge—not a replacement for formal VET teacher education, but a feasible starting point for institutions facing the immediate operational reality of placing unprepared teachers in UAV classrooms.

The model also carries implications for VET teacher continuing education credit systems.

It should be acknowledged that "zero-cost" refers strictly to financial expenditure at the institutional level, not to the absence of resource requirements altogether. The model imposes non-trivial costs in time (five years of sustained self-study), basic digital infrastructure (a computing device and internet connection), and English-language proficiency sufficient to interact with AI agent interfaces that are predominantly English-medium. For teachers in remote vocational colleges where internet access is intermittent or digital literacy is developing, these implicit costs may represent significant barriers. Furthermore, the model assumes a baseline level of self-directed learning orientation—the capacity and motivation to sustain independent study over extended periods without external accountability structures—that may not be uniformly distributed across VET teacher populations. These caveats do not diminish the model's practical value but should temper claims of universal applicability: the NFSC+Agent approach is a pragmatic bridge for VET teachers who possess sufficient digital access, self-directed learning capacity, and institutional tolerance—conditions that are widely but not universally satisfied. In China's higher vocational education sector, teachers are required to accumulate continuing education credits through approved professional development activities. A self-directed model with demonstrable outputs (course syllabi, platform development, teaching reform research) could potentially be recognised within such credit frameworks, reducing the burden on teachers who currently must balance full teaching loads with off-site training attendance. Internationally, the model's alignment with the European Centre for the Development of Vocational Training (Cedefop) competency framework—which identifies "managing one's own professional development" and "integrating digital technologies into teaching-learning processes" as core VET teacher competencies—suggests potential for cross-system adaptation. More specifically, the NFSC+Agent model operationalises Cedefop's competency descriptors at three levels. At the individual teacher level, the structured self-study protocol substitutes for absent institutional mentoring, addressing what Cedefop terms the "dimensions of teacher professional learning that institutions must provide but frequently cannot" (Cedefop, 2022). At the institutional level, the zero-cost deployment architecture removes financial barriers that European frameworks identify as "one of the most persistent obstacles to VET teacher continuing professional development" (OECD, 2021). At the policy level, the model's emphasis on demonstrable outputs—course syllabi, teaching platforms, reform research—aligns with competency-based accreditation mechanisms increasingly adopted in both European and Asian VET systems. The Chinese UAV VET context, with its compressed programme expansion timeline and acute teacher shortage, represents an extreme case that stress-tests the model's transferability: if the approach functions under these conditions, its applicability to less extreme VET staffing scenarios in other national contexts becomes plausible. A systematic comparison with the ASEAN Qualifications Reference Framework (AQRF) and its implications for VET teacher mobility represents a productive direction for future research.

### 5.5 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the interpretation and generalisability of findings. First, the sampling strategy—purposive and convenience-based within Sichuan province—limits external validity. Peer teachers and administrators were recruited from institutions within a single provincial administrative region with its own industrial structure and policy environment, which may not represent the conditions of UAV VET in other Chinese provinces (e.g., Guangdong, Jiangsu) or in other countries. Second, all survey instruments rely on self-reported perceptions, which are subject to social desirability bias and may diverge from objective measures of teaching quality or student learning outcomes. The study does not include direct observation of peer teachers' subsequent attempts to implement the model, nor does it track student achievement outcomes beyond those documented in the teaching archives.

Third, the teaching archive component constitutes a single-case design (N = 1 teacher). While the richness of the 10-semester longitudinal record provides internal validity for claims about this teacher's developmental trajectory, it cannot support causal attribution of that trajectory exclusively to the NFSC+Agent model. Alternative explanations—Teacher Z's individual aptitude, motivation, prior disciplinary knowledge, or institutional support conditions—cannot be excluded. Fourth, the study does not employ a randomised controlled trial or quasi-experimental comparison group design. As such, the evidence supports an exploratory description of how the model functioned in one context, rather than a causal demonstration of its superiority over alternative professional development approaches.

It is emphasised that this study is positioned as an exploratory mixed-methods investigation, not a causal intervention study. Its contribution lies in documenting and empirically characterising a phenomenon—technology-assisted self-directed cross-disciplinary VET teacher development—that has not been systematically described in the literature, and in developing and field-testing a multi-stakeholder evaluation framework that can be applied in future, methodologically stronger designs.

## §6 Conclusion

This study reported the design and preliminary framing of a mixed-methods empirical investigation of the NFSC+Agent self-directed professional development model for cross-disciplinary vocational teachers in Chinese UAV education. Guided by two research questions, the study examined how the model supports cross-disciplinary VET teacher development (RQ1) and how external peer teachers perceive its transferability (RQ2). At the time of writing, questionnaire-based data collection from three stakeholder groups—institutional administrators (N ≥ 20), peer teachers (N ≥ 40), and students (N ≥ 60)—is pending, scheduled for June–July 2026. Longitudinal teaching archives spanning ten semesters (2021–2025) provide the process evidence base, documenting a complete developmental trajectory from a single pilot course to an eight-course curriculum with an integrated zero-cost web learning portal.

The study makes three contributions to the VET research literature. First, it provides the first systematic empirical characterisation of a self-directed, technology-assisted professional development model specifically designed for cross-disciplinary VET teachers—a population that the literature has recognised as structurally significant but has rarely studied as a distinct professional development target group. Second, it introduces a multi-stakeholder, multi-instrument evaluation framework combining peer teacher evaluation (seven-dimension custom instrument), student usability metrics (SUS and TAM), and institutional context profiling, offering a methodological template for future investigations of teacher development interventions in VET settings. Third, it identifies provisional transfer conditions—anchoring knowledge domain, structured self-study protocol, and institutional tolerance—that inform practical decisions about model adoption in other VET institutions and disciplines.

Future research should pursue three directions. Multi-centre validation across Chinese provinces with different industrial profiles would assess the model's generalisability beyond Sichuan's specific UAV ecosystem. A quasi-experimental or randomised controlled trial design comparing the NFSC+Agent model against standard institutional professional development (or a waitlist control) would strengthen causal inference. Longitudinal tracking of teachers who adopt the model—monitoring course development trajectories, student outcomes, and sustained platform use over three or more years—would illuminate the durability and evolution of self-directed professional development in VET contexts.

The NFSC+Agent model does not claim to replace formal VET teacher education programmes, which provide the disciplinary depth, pedagogical foundation, and professional socialisation that self-directed learning cannot reproduce. Rather, it offers a pragmatic bridge: a structured, zero-cost pathway for the thousands of cross-disciplinary vocational teachers who, at this moment, are required to teach technical subjects for which they were not formally prepared and who lack access to the professional development resources that would ideally support them. In a VET landscape characterised by rapid programme expansion, structural teacher shortages, and persistent resource constraints, such pragmatic bridges deserve empirical attention.
