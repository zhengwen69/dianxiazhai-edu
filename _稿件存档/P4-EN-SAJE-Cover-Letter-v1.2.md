**Significance and Novelty**

Cross-disciplinary teaching assignments—where vocational instructors are required to teach subjects outside their formal qualification—constitute a systemic challenge in developing-country education systems. While the phenomenon is widespread, documented cases of teachers successfully navigating such transitions through self-directed means are virtually absent from the literature. This paper reports what is, to our knowledge, the first longitudinal case study tracing a vocational teacher's trajectory from disciplinary novice to research-active programme leader across more than a decade, facilitated by a zero-cost, self-directed learning model.

Three features distinguish this contribution:

1. **Empirical depth**: The study draws on four years of continuous teaching journals, system usage records, and verifiable institutional outputs—including three funded research projects and a copyright registration—providing unusually rich process data for a teacher development study.

2. **Cross-domain evidence**: While the primary case focuses on UAV technology teaching, three well-documented auxiliary cases spanning environmental engineering transitions and traditional music self-study provide preliminary evidence of transferability across disciplines and life stages.

3. **Policy relevance for developing contexts**: The self-directed model operates at zero marginal institutional cost, requires no specialised IT infrastructure, and has been deployed across eight languages—characteristics that address the resource constraints documented in SAJE's recent publications on South African schools (e.g., Chuene & Teane, 2024; Rasool & Naidoo, 2024; Naidoo, 2026).

**Fit with SAJE**

The manuscript speaks directly to the journal's stated interest in "education research where the education system is characterised by transformation, and/or an emerging economy/development state, and/or scarce resources." We trace the implications of the model for teacher training policy in developing countries, with particular reference to the South African TVET college sector and the BRICS cooperation framework. The paper's focus on the professional growth of a practising teacher aligns with SAJE's scope of accepting research on "the training of undergraduate/postgraduate teachers."
