## Key Strengths

- **Timely, policy-relevant topic**: Cross-disciplinary teaching assignments are a structural challenge across developing-country vocational systems, and the paper addresses a genuine gap in both scholarship and practice.
- **Rich longitudinal data**: Four years of continuous teaching journals (45,000 Chinese characters) provide process-level insight into teacher development rarely available in the literature.
- **Strong SAJE engagement**: Six SAJE-published articles are cited and integrated into the argument, demonstrating genuine engagement with the journal's conversation rather than token referencing.
- **Honest limitations**: The paper candidly acknowledges N=1, self-report bias, absence of student outcome data, and the researcher-developer conflict of interest—a level of reflexivity unusual in practitioner research.
- **Concrete policy implications**: The zero-cost architecture and BRICS/South-South framing give the paper direct practical relevance for SAJE's readership in developing-country education systems.

## Critical Issues (ranked by severity)

### 1. Triangulation claims exceed the evidence (Methodology — SEVERE)

The paper claims data triangulation across four sources (§3.3), but three of these—teaching journals, system usage records, and course design documents—originate from Teacher Z. The institutional outputs (funded projects, copyright registration) provide independent confirmation of *achievement* but do not triangulate the *learning process* that is the paper's central phenomenon. The triangulation is best characterised as multi-source single-informant corroboration, not the independent-source triangulation that qualitative methodologies require (Patton, 2015; Lincoln & Guba, 1985). This should be honestly recharacterised in §3.3 and §3.4, and the interpretive claims should be tempered accordingly.

### 2. Auxiliary case evidence is insufficient for RQ2 transferability claims (Methodology — SEVERE)

RQ2 asks about conditions for cross-disciplinary and cross-institutional transfer, yet the three auxiliary cases receive no analytical treatment in §4 (Results). They are described in one paragraph of §3.6 (methodology) and one paragraph of §5.2 (discussion), with no evidence trail from data to themes. The claim that "three conditions for successful transfer cut across all four cases" (§5.2) is asserted rather than demonstrated. Teacher H's case (retired engineer learning flute music) is particularly problematic: the leap from vocational UAV teaching to recreational music learning rests on an analogy (both use NFSC) rather than on evidence that the *conditions* for vocational cross-disciplinary teaching transfer to non-vocational, non-teaching contexts. Either expand the auxiliary case analysis with proper evidence or substantially narrow the transferability claims.

### 3. Thematic analysis lacks methodological transparency (Methodology — MODERATE)

Braun & Clarke (2006) is cited and three analytical stages are described, but the paper provides no inter-coder reliability coefficient, no member-checking procedure, no audit trail from raw journal data to themes, and no indication of how the 45,000-character corpus was reduced to the three patterns reported in §4.5. For comparison, the authors' own NFSC paper for EJEE reports Cohen's κ = .74 on a subset of student outputs—a standard of transparency not met here. The absence of these quality markers undermines confidence in the reported themes, particularly given the researcher-developer dual role.

### 4. Researcher-developer conflict of interest (Positionality — MODERATE)

The authors developed the NFSC methodology they evaluate. This is noted in §5.1 ("We acknowledge the risk of confirmation bias… we triangulated self-report data against independent evidence") but the mitigation is insufficient: the "independent evidence" (funded projects, copyright) does not speak to the learning process. An independent analyst coding a subset of journal entries would meaningfully address this concern. Without it, the paper reads as developer testimony rather than independent evaluation.

### 5. Theory contribution is overstated relative to empirical base (Theory — MODERATE)

The paper claims to extend self-directed professional development theory from "curriculum practice enhancement" to "foundational disciplinary knowledge acquisition" (§5.1, §6). This is a legitimate extension, but it is incremental rather than transformative. The three transfer conditions identified (§5.2—anchoring knowledge domain, structured self-study protocol, institutional permission) are well-established in adult learning theory (Knowles, Brookfield, Billett) and do not constitute novel theoretical propositions. The paper would be stronger if it positioned its contribution as primarily empirical and practical rather than theoretical, or if it engaged more deeply with theoretical frameworks it cites but does not use (Engeström's expansive learning, Shulman's PCK).

## Minor Issues

1. **Redundancy across sections**: The Abstract, §1 (Introduction), and §6 (Conclusion) substantially overlap in describing Teacher Z's trajectory and the NFSC model. Streamlining would improve readability and free word count for methodological detail.

2. **Figures are substandard for publication**: Figure 1 is an ASCII text diagram. For a journal publication, this should be converted to a proper graphic. SAJE typically requires publication-quality figures.

3. **The 10 UAV courses are listed but not analysed** (§4.2): Listing all 10 course titles occupies space without analytical payoff. Consider a table showing how course difficulty or domain type maps onto developmental phases.

4. **Engeström (2001) and Shulman (1987) are cited but unused**: Both appear in the reference list but are never engaged in the theoretical framework or discussion. Either integrate them into the argument or remove them.

5. **"Scene" dimension of NFSC under-described**: The NFSC acronym (Narrative-Framework-Scene-Creation) implies four components, but the "Scene" component receives minimal description in the context of self-directed teacher learning. How does scenario-based learning function when the learner is alone with an agent system, without peers or an instructor?

6. **Missing student learning outcome data**: This is acknowledged in §5.4, but the paper's central claim—that the model "supports cross-disciplinary teaching competence"—is weakened by the absence of any measure of teaching effectiveness beyond institutional outputs (funded projects, copyright). Even indirect indicators (student evaluation scores, certification pass rates) would strengthen the argument, and Table 1 mentions "student certification pass rates" under 2024 as external recognition—why are these not reported?

7. **Minor language issues**: (a) §1 para 3: "returning to university for a second degree demands time and money that working teachers… simply do not have"—consider "may not have" for epistemological humility; (b) §4.1: the translated journal excerpt ("I am not starting from zero…") is evocative but a single excerpt cannot carry the weight of the "analogical transfer" claim; (c) several paragraphs in §5 read as advocacy rather than scholarly inference.

8. **Ethical considerations (§3.5) are thin**: "The study involved no vulnerable populations and posed minimal risk" is a conclusion, not a description of ethical safeguards. Was Teacher Z, as the research subject, also a co-author? The dual role of subject and researcher requires explicit discussion.

## Overlap Check with P1 and P2

### P4+ vs. P1 (NFSC-EJEE): No paragraph-level repetition detected

P1 describes the NFSC pedagogy as a four-dimensional classroom teaching model for graduate engineering education, grounded in the same theoretical traditions (Bruner, constructive alignment, scenario-based learning). P4+ describes NFSC as a self-directed learning methodology repurposed for individual teacher development. The two papers:

- Share conceptual DNA (NFSC framework, Bruner, Biggs, the Mismatch-Repair framework)
- Do **not** share any paragraph-level text or verbatim passages
- Apply NFSC to different domains (classroom pedagogy vs. self-directed teacher learning)
- Target different journals (EJEE vs. SAJE)
- Ask different research questions

The conceptual overlap is inherent to a multi-paper research programme and is acceptable. The NFSC description in P4+ (§4.1) is functional and brief (two paragraphs), while P1 devotes its full §3 to the pedagogical architecture. No self-plagiarism concern.

## Reviewer Recommendation to Editor

**Recommendation: Minor Revision**

This manuscript addresses a genuinely important and under-researched problem: how vocational teachers assigned outside their qualification area can develop teaching competence without formal training. The longitudinal case study provides rich process data that the teacher professional development literature lacks, and the policy implications for developing-country vocational systems are concrete and well-argued. The paper's engagement with SAJE-published work is commendable and positions the contribution within the journal's ongoing conversation about self-directed professional development (Verster et al., 2024), ICT in education (Naidoo, 2026; Rasool & Naidoo, 2024), and curriculum implementation in resource-constrained settings (Chuene & Teane, 2024).

However, the manuscript has three methodological weaknesses that require revision before acceptance:

1. **Triangulation is claimed but not demonstrated.** The four data sources are largely co-located in Teacher Z, and the paper should honestly recharacterise its evidence base as multi-source single-informant rather than independent-source triangulated. Interpretive claims should be tempered accordingly.

2. **RQ2 transferability claims outrun the auxiliary case evidence.** Three auxiliary cases mentioned in one paragraph each cannot sustain claims about cross-disciplinary and cross-institutional transfer. Either expand the auxiliary case analysis or narrow the claims.

3. **Thematic analysis needs methodological transparency.** An inter-coder reliability coefficient, member-checking procedure, or audit trail from raw data to themes should be reported. This is particularly important given the researcher-developer dual role.

These issues are fixable. The paper's core empirical contribution—a documented four-year trajectory from disciplinary novice to research-active programme leader—is valuable and deserves publication. The revisions requested are within scope for a minor revision cycle.

I encourage the authors to resist the temptation to overclaim. The paper's strength lies in its empirical specificity and practical relevance, not in theoretical novelty. Positioning the contribution as a well-documented case with transferable design principles—rather than as a validated model—would strengthen the manuscript's credibility and impact.
